Government Structure

The Executive Branch

The President

- Elected through score voting, with voters ranking candidates from 1 to 10.

- The Electoral College is now a body that certifies the results of the Presidential Election.

- In the event of a tie, both the President and the Vice President are elected by a joint session of Congress presided over by the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court.

- Day-to-day administration is delegated to a figure known as the First Secretary, explained in further detail in their own section.

- The President's role as commander-in-chief, powers over appointment, veto power, and ability to declare and manage states of emergency cannot be delegated to the First Secretary.

- The impoundment of Congressionally allocated funds may be done, but is subject to the approval of the House of Representatives within 60 days.

- The Congress' ability to grant the President extraordinary powers upon the proclamation of a National Emergency is heavily restricted. Declarations must draw from Congressional authorizations, be narrow in scope and duration, submitted to Congress, proportional to the crisis, and narrowly tailored to restrict Constitutional rights in as limited a fashion as possible. They are subject to judicial review.

- The Congress' ability to grant the President extraordinary powers upon the proclamation of a National Emergency is heavily restricted. Declarations must draw from Congressional authorizations, be narrow in scope and duration, proportional to the crisis, and narrowly tailored to restrict Constitutional rights in as limited a fashion as possible. They are subject to both judicial review and Congressional amendment.

- If the Armed Forces is introduced into hostilities without a declaration of war, the President must provide a report to Congress, who must approve the conflict within 60 days.

- The Line-Item veto is explicitly prohibited.

- The Pocket Veto is explicitly prohibited.

The First Secretary

- The chief advisor of the President of the United States and leader of the Presidential Cabinet, appointed by the President with the advice and consent of the House of Representatives. The rest of the cabinet is appointed by the President with the advice and consent of the First Secretary.

- Manages the day-to-day administration of the State, including the implementation of laws, direction of the Federal Bureaucracy, and management of federal personnel and resources.

- The House of Representatives may retract their consent through a motion to vacate, which forces the resignation of the First Secretary. The House will then consent to a new First Secretary within 60 days, during which time the President will run an interim administration. If the House somehow can't consent to a new First Secretary within 6 months, a special election for the House of Representatives will be called.

The Federal Bureaucracy

- Bureaucratic agencies can be insulated from executive control by Congress.

- Bureaucratic agencies can be delegated regulatory powers by Congress, provided Congress provides clear standards, guidelines, and scope of authority for their power.

- Bureaucratic agencies must be staffed on the basis of merit, experience and competence.

- Bureaucratic agencies must conduct their operations openly and transparently except where disclosure would harm operation or jeapordize national security.

Executive Secretaries

- Executive Secretaries are senior officers in the executive branch appointed for 6 year terms by 2/3rds of the Senate.

- The Attorney General is the chief law enforcement officer of the United States, responsible for federal law enforcement and prosecutions, including that of government officials.

- The Treasurer oversees the collection of revenues, management of public debt, and the US mint

- The Inspector General of the United States investigates waste, fraud and abuse in the government and oversees all other Inspectors General.

- The Comptroller General is the chief auditor of the Federal Government, ensuring accountability in the usage of public funds.

- The Secretary of State is responsible for the administration of Federal elections, the certification, publication, and dissemination of all federal documents, and the administration of the naturalization process.

- The Chief Statistician of the United States is responsible for the administration of the entire Federal Statistical System of the United States, including the Census.

- The Surgeon General of the United States is responsible for the administration of federal public health agencies.

- The Senate may create additional Executive Secretaries.

- The Treasurer, Attorney General, Inspector General, Comptroller General, and Secretary of State are on an Ethics Commission charged with keeping the Federal Government in line with a Federal Code of Conduct created by Congress. Recommendations by the Ethics Commission can lower the threshhold required for Congressional expulsion and enable Judicial self expulsion.

The Legislative Branch

- Bills introduced in Congress can only have a single subject.

- A referendum on a law will be held next general election given the signature of at least seven percent of voters in the last general election (with at least half the States having one-fifth of one percent of their population being signatories).

- One appropriations bill must be passed every fiscal year. All taxes also must be re-authorized every year in a single bill.

- If the budget is not passed by the beginning of the fiscal year, last year's budget is simply used instead.

- Appropriations and taxes that fall outside of their yearly bills require a three-fifths majority to pass.

- Congress now has full control over their own "Legislative Marshals", which shall protect the capitol complex and act as an enforcer of last resort for Congressional directives (e.g. subpoenas).

The House of Representatives

- The number of seats in Congress is now apportioned via the cube root law, thereby increasing the number of Representatives to 693.

- Only one seat per electoral district is allowed.

- Representatives are now elected via Instant Runoff voting.

- Representatives can now be subject to recall elections, which will be held given a petition of 20% of eligable voters.

- A standardized rule for vacancies in the House of Representatives is established to ensure fairness.

- Electoral districts must be redrawn by independent redistricting commissions. Gerrymandering is explicitly prohibited.

The Senate

- The Seventeenth Amendment is repealed. States now have full control over how their Senator is elected.

- Senators must have resided in the State they represent for at least 10 years.

- Ex-Presidents (Presidents Emeritus) are made Senators for life upon leaving Presidential office, but do not count towards the quorum.

- The Senate may no longer introduce bills. Bills sent from the House of Representatives may only be rejected a maximum of two times in the case of annual appropriation and taxation bills and a maximum of five times otherwise. The Senate may only stall a bill a maximum of 60 days for annual appropriation and taxation bills and a maximum of 120 days otherwise.

The Judiciary

- The Marshals Service is now under the control of the judiciary, with a decentralized structure.

- The Judicial Conference of the United States is now a Constitutional body that promotes judicial self governance.

- The current judicial structure of inferior courts of the United States is now Constitutionally ingrained, though Congress can still modify their number, composition, and jurisdiction.

- A Federal Claims Court is established to handle lawsuits against the Federal government.

The Supreme Court

- The Supreme Court focuses primarily on the adjudication of matters of common & natural law due to the existence of the other courts.

- Justices are appointed via the consent of two-thirds of the Senate.

- The number of Justices is permanently locked at 9.

The Constitutional Court

- The Constitutional Court is a body that focuses primarily on the Constitutional review of governments and other Constitutional questions.

- There is one justice for each circuit court appointed by majority vote of all district and circuit court judges of each circuit. Justices serve twelve year terms, staggered by four years in thirds.

The Electoral Court

- The Electoral Court is focused primarily on federal election law and the adjudication of election results.

- Justices are selected by two thirds majority of the Judicial Conference of the United States with the advice and consent of the Secretary of State from active judges experienced in election law who have not engaged in partisan political activity for at least five years prior to appointment.

- All Presidential election results are adjudicated by the Electoral Court before being sent for certification by the Electoral College and Secretary of State.

Federalism

- Congress has the power to enforce establish standards for welfare programs, with States having the power to implement them, with flexibility to exceed standards set by Congress.

- Congress has the power to construct infastructure with consent from the affected State.

- Indian Tribes have the right to self governance as established by treaty ratified by Congress.

- Federal lands are divided into disposable and permanent lands. Congress has the power to manage all Federal lands, may dispose of Permanent Federal lands with two-thirds vote, and may delegate any aspect of the governance of federal lands to States. States must recieve compensation for permanent public lands exempt from state taxation.

- Congress has the power to promulgate economic regulations in partnership with the several States.

- Congress has the power to regulate campaign financing for Federal elections and the power to regulate the conduct of the Presidential election.

- All treaties ratified by Congress shall be the supreme law of the land, equal in force to Federal statutes.

- The United States has the power to regulate firearms in cases of interstate commerce to ensure compliance with State laws and create background checks for interstate firearm transfers. It is otherwise a state issue.

- Municipalities of the United States may manage and accquire public services.

- The District of Colombia has all rights and responsibilities of a State except Congressional representation.

Rights

- Equality under the law, the right of adult citizens to vote, and lawful access to public services may not be abridged by any arbitrary basis (baking a gay wedding cake is not a public service shut up).

- People have the right to roam responsibly on public lands.

- People have the right to freely associate, which includes the right to collective bargaining.

- All work performed in the jurisidiction of the United States is voluntary and fairly compensated.

- The rights protected by the Constitution are the rights of natural persons only.

- People have a right to privacy and autonomy over personal decisions.

- Equality under the law applies to all persons in the United States regardless of citizenship status.

- People have a right to own property. The federal government cannot take land for public use without just compensation. The US cannot impose unreasonable restrictions on the ownership or use of property.

- People are allowed to sue the Federal government in a lot more scenarios.
